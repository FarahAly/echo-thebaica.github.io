Luxor Temple

Constructed primarily around 1400 BCE by Amenhotep III and later expanded by Ramses II, Luxor Temple stands elegantly in the heart of modern-day Luxor. Unlike other prominent temples in ancient Thebes, it was not dedicated to a specific cult god or a deified version of the king in death. Instead, it was dedicated to the rejuvenation of kingship, and historians believe it may have been the site where many of Egypt's pharaohs were symbolically crowned. The temple served as the focal point for the annual Opet Festival, a grand religious celebration during which the cult statues of Amun, Mut, and Khonsu were paraded down the majestic Avenue of Sphinxes from Karnak to Luxor Temple. The site is also a unique historical tapestry, preserving Roman shrines, Christian frescoes, and the active Abu Haggag Mosque, which was built directly atop the ancient columns before the temple was excavated.
