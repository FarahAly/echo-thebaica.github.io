Colossi of Memnon

The Colossi of Memnon are two towering, faceless statues of Pharaoh Amenhotep III that sit isolated in the fields of the West Bank of Luxor. Standing at an imposing 18 meters tall and carved from blocks of quartzite sandstone hauled all the way from modern-day Cairo, they originally stood as the majestic guardians at the entrance of Amenhotep's mortuary temple. While the statues remain, the temple behind them—once the largest in Egypt—was entirely destroyed over millennia by Nile floods, earthquakes, and stone thieves.The statues gained immense fame in antiquity following a massive earthquake in 27 BCE that shattered the northern statue. Following this damage, the statue supposedly began to emit a high-pitched "singing" or whistling sound at dawn, likely caused by rising temperatures and evaporating dew passing through the cracks in the stone. Greek and Roman tourists flocked to hear the "Vocal Memnon," though the sound ceased after the statue was repaired by the Roman Emperor Septimius Severus in the 3rd century.
