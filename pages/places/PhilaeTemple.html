Philae Temple

Originally located on Philae Island near the First Cataract of the Nile, this stunning temple complex was entirely dedicated to the goddess Isis, the mythological mother of Horus and symbol of healing and magic. Built primarily during the Ptolemaic period with later Roman additions, it represents the last major outpost of ancient Egyptian religion; pagan worship continued here well into the 6th century CE, long after the rest of the empire had converted to Christianity. The island's architecture is a beautiful blend of Egyptian tradition and Greco-Roman styling. Like Abu Simbel, the Philae Temple was rescued from the rising waters of the Nile following the construction of the Aswan dams. In an impressive UNESCO project in the 1970s, the entire complex was dismantled stone by stone and meticulously reassembled on the nearby, higher-elevation Agilkia Island, which was even reshaped to match the original island's footprint. Today, it remains famous for its romantic island setting, the elegant Kiosk of Trajan, and a spectacular evening Sound and Light show.
