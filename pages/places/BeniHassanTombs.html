Beni Hassan Tombs

Perched on the limestone cliffs of the eastern bank of the Nile in Middle Egypt, the Beni Hasan rock-cut tombs are a crucial archaeological site dating to the Middle Kingdom (mostly the 11th and 12th Dynasties). Unlike the grand royal tombs of Luxor, these tombs were built for powerful regional governors, or "nomarchs," reflecting a period in Egyptian history when power was heavily decentralized away from the ruling pharaohs. The tombs are accessible via a steep climb but offer commanding views of the Nile Valley below.The interiors of the tombs are famous for their lively, vividly painted frescoes that focus heavily on daily life rather than purely religious or funerary texts. The walls are covered in highly detailed, sequential depictions of hunting, agriculture, weaving, and most notably, an intricate series of hundreds of wrestling holds and military exercises. These secular scenes provide historians with an unparalleled window into the everyday activities and martial arts of ancient Egyptian society.
