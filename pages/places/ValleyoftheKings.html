Valley of the Kings

Hidden within the craggy limestone hills of the Theban Necropolis on the West Bank of Luxor, the Valley of the Kings served as the premier burial ground for pharaohs and powerful nobles of the New Kingdom for nearly 500 years. The secluded wadi was chosen for its natural pyramid-shaped peak and its isolated geography, which was intended to protect the tombs from theft. Today, the valley contains over 60 discovered tombs, ranging from simple pits to massive underground complexes with over 120 chambers. The subterranean tombs are celebrated for their remarkably preserved wall paintings and intricate reliefs that depict scenes from ancient Egyptian funerary texts, such as the Book of the Dead and the Amduat. These texts were meant to guide the deceased pharaoh safely through the underworld to achieve eternal life. The valley is most famous worldwide as the resting place of Tutankhamun, whose nearly intact tomb was discovered in 1922.
